## Job Market Paper

<p class="paper-title">
Weather Shocks, Labor Reallocation, and Structural Transformation: Micro Evidence from Africa
</p>

<p class="paper-note">Job Market Paper</p>

Structural transformation, which is the movement of labor from agriculture to more productive non-agricultural activities, is a key driver of economic development. Yet labor released from agriculture does not always transition into productive non-agricultural employment. This paper develops a structural framework that decomposes structural transformation into three sequential stages: labor release, labor reallocation, and labor absorption, allowing the roles of subsistence constraints, mobility barriers, land transferability, and labor absorption capacity to be separately identified. Using household panel data from the World Bank Living Standards Measurement Study (LSMS) for Niger, Malawi, and Ethiopia, combined with high-resolution climate and geospatial data, I first provide reduced-form evidence on how weather-induced agricultural productivity shocks interact with these constraints to shape household labor allocation. I then estimate the structural model using the simulated method of moments and conduct counterfactual policy analysis. The results reveal substantial heterogeneity in the mechanisms governing structural transformation. Subsistence constraints are the primary barrier in Niger and Malawi, whereas labor reallocation and labor absorption constrain structural transformation in Ethiopia. By identifying the binding constraints at each stage of structural transformation, the proposed framework provides a unified approach for understanding labor allocation and evaluating development policies.

## Working Papers

<p class="paper-title">
Can the Service Sector Lead Structural Transformation in Africa? Evidence from Côte d'Ivoire
</p>

<p class="paper-note">with Jeremy Foltz</p>

**Paper:** [SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6530951)

Standard models of structural transformation typically view the expansion of manufacturing as a central stage of economic development. Many African countries, however, have moved directly toward service-based economies, raising questions about the implications for labor absorption and productivity growth. This paper asks whether the service sector can lead structural transformation in an African economy through productivity growth and labor reallocation. Using unique firm-level panel data from Côte d'Ivoire, one of Africa's fast-growing "leopard" economies, we estimate total factor productivity and examine labor movements across firms with different productivity levels. We find that average total factor productivity in services is 2.46 times as high as in manufacturing. High-productivity service firms also hire more workers overall, especially unskilled workers, than low-productivity service firms. These results suggest that the service sector can contribute meaningfully to structural transformation and GDP growth in Africa, with important implications for development policy.

## Work in Progress

<p class="paper-title">
Gender Constraints and Labor Allocation during Structural Transformation
</p>

Structural transformation—labor reallocation from low-productivity agriculture to more productive manufacturing and services—is a central driver of economic growth in low- and middle-income countries. While recent work (Buera et al., 2011; Buera & Shin, 2013) highlights household-level constraints that can limit labor reallocation, these constraints may be particularly binding for women. Female participation in non-agricultural sectors remains low and has stagnated across much of Sub-Saharan Africa (Backhaus & Loichinger, 2022; Asaleye & Strydom, 2024). However, it remains unclear whether these gender differences reflect preferences or binding constraints. This paper asks: Do gender differences in labor allocation reflect preferences or constraints, and to what extent do gender-specific constraints limit women’s labor reallocation during structural transformation?
We construct individual panel data for four African countries (Niger, Nigeria, Malawi, and Ethiopia) using the World Bank’s LSMS surveys, combined with high-resolution satellite data on NDVI (MODIS), rainfall (CHIRPS), and temperature (ERA-LAND). We develop an exogenous and continuous measure of weather-driven agricultural productivity by predicting NDVI growth from rainfall and temperature using a Random Forest model. We then model sectoral transitions as the outcome of a household decision problem in which men and women face identical wages but gender-specific entry costs into non-agricultural sectors. By interacting gender with the productivity measure, we identify heterogeneity in these entry costs.
We find that women respond more strongly than men to increases in weather-induced agricultural productivity by transitioning into non-agricultural sectors. This asymmetric response suggests that women face binding entry constraints in low-productivity states that are partially relaxed when agricultural productivity improves. The results are inconsistent with purely preference-driven differences and instead point to gender-specific barriers to mobility. These findings imply that such constraints can significantly slow structural transformation by limiting women’s movement into more productive sectors.


<p class="paper-title">
Climate Change and Conflict: Causal Evidence using New Machine Learning Methods
</p>

<p class="paper-note">with Jeremy Foltz</p>

Climate-conflict studies have produced widely divergent estimates of the effects of climate shocks on violent conflict. We argue that this heterogeneity reflects three dimensions: the type of climate shock, spatial interference between neighboring locations, and ecological heterogeneity across land systems. Using a geospatial panel covering Africa between 2000 and 2020, we combine conflict records, satellite-derived climate measures, and land-cover classifications to estimate causal effects with Double/Debiased Machine Learning (DDML). To account for interference, we develop an exposure mapping that jointly characterizes a location's own climate shock and neighboring exposure, enabling estimation of direct and spillover treatment effects. We find that rainfall shocks generate conflict primarily through spillover channels, whereas temperature shocks mainly affect directly exposed locations. Moreover, these effects differ systematically across ecological systems: neighboring droughts increase conflict in cropland and drylands, neighboring rainfall surpluses reduce conflict in shrublands and deserts, heat shocks lower conflicts in forests and grasslands, and cold shocks increase conflict in cropland, grasslands, and drylands. Our results demonstrate that accounting for spatial spillovers and econlogical heterogeneity substantially improves understanding of climate-conflict dynamics and helps explain why previous studies reach conflicting conclusions.
